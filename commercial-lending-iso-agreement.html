What Is an ISO Agreement in Commercial Finance

An ISO agreement—or Independent Sales Organization agreement—in commercial finance is a contract between a lender or financing advisory firm and a referral partner (broker, ISO, vendor, consultant, or other deal source). It defines how introductions are made, how compensation is calculated and paid, and how both parties protect their interests.

These agreements are also commonly called broker referral agreements, lender ISO agreements, or commercial lending referral agreements. Regardless of the name, they serve the same purpose: establishing a formal partnership for referring business loan and financing opportunities.

Key elements typically include revenue share or commission structure, referral ownership, non-circumvention, clawback provisions, prospect protection periods, and independent contractor status.

Why Lenders Use Referral Agreements

Lenders and financing firms use referral agreements for several reasons:

  • Clear expectations—Both parties understand how compensation works, when payment is made, and under what conditions.
  • Referral ownership—The agreement establishes who introduced the prospect and when that introduction occurred, reducing disputes.
  • Protection for both parties—Non-circumvention and prospect protection prevent either side from bypassing the other after an introduction.
  • Compliance and risk management—Agreements often require referrers to confirm they are legally permitted to receive compensation and to comply with employer policies and applicable law.
  • Clawback provisions—If a funded deal later defaults or is charged back, the agreement defines how commissions are handled.

Without a signed agreement, referral partners typically cannot submit deals. The agreement is the foundation of the partnership.

Industry Standard

Key Terms in a Typical ISO Agreement

Revenue Share

The percentage of gross commission or revenue the referrer receives when a referred transaction successfully funds. Typically expressed as a percentage of what the lender or financing firm actually receives.

Referral Ownership

Establishes who introduced the prospect and when. Defines the referrer's right to compensation for that introduction and prevents confusion about who "owns" the referral.

Non-Circumvention

Protects both parties from being bypassed after an introduction. Typically restricts how introduced prospects and funding-source relationships may be used outside the agreement.

Clawback Provisions

If a funded transaction later defaults, is rescinded, charged back, or causes the financing firm to return commission, the referrer may be required to return their share. Standard in commercial lending.

Prospect Protection

The period during which an introduced prospect is "protected"—meaning the referrer retains rights to compensation if that prospect closes with the lender or financing firm. Often 24–60 months from introduction.

Independent Contractor Status

The referrer participates as an independent contractor, not an employee or partner. Clarifies tax treatment and limits the lender's liability for the referrer's actions.
