What's Different About Financing a Franchise

Franchises carry an advantage most small businesses don't: a proven model and brand lenders can underwrite against.

Lenders view established franchise systems as lower risk because the unit economics are documented across many locations. That can mean better terms for franchise buyers—especially for recognized brands on an approved franchisor list. Financing typically funds the franchise fee, build-out, equipment, and opening working capital, whether you're buying your first unit or adding to an existing group.

SBA loans are a common fit for franchises (many brands are SBA-registered), but they're not the only path. Newer franchisees, deals that need to close faster than SBA timelines allow, or buyers who don't fit bank credit often use equipment financing and working capital instead.

Financing Across Industries

Franchise or independent, the financing playbook adapts to how a business earns and what it owns.

The product that fits depends on the industry's cash-flow shape and assets:

  • Restaurants & hospitality—equipment financing for kitchens plus working capital for seasonal swings.
  • Retail & e-commerce—inventory financing and revenue-based capital tied to sales cycles.
  • Trucking & logistics—equipment financing for rigs and factoring to cover the gap until freight invoices pay.
  • Manufacturing & contractors—machinery financing and purchase-order or A/R funding for large jobs.
  • Healthcare & professional practices—practice acquisition and equipment loans against steady patient revenue.
  • Auto repair, cleaning, landscaping & service businesses—equipment and working capital sized to the route or shop.

Financing With Weaker Credit or a Prior Decline

A bank decline or a lower credit score doesn't end the search. Revenue-based financing, equipment loans (where the asset is collateral), and factoring (which leans on your customers' credit) all routinely fund businesses banks pass on. What matters most is current revenue, consistent deposits, and a clear use of funds.

If a deal has already been declined, it can be routed to alternative lenders for declined borrowers whose criteria fit the profile—so a "no" from one lender becomes a "yes" somewhere better matched.
